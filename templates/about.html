Optimizing ICU Management by Predicting Intensive Care Urgency (IC-U)

What is the problem?

Over 5 million patients annually require some form of intensive care, which requires continual and often invasive monitoring, as well as near 1-to-1 nurse-to-patient staffing in order to treat patients effectively. As a result of the complicated nature of ICU care, ICUs are one of the leading drivers of hospital costs in the US today. Hospitals are currently spending $80B a year in ICU care, and these costs are expected to double by 2030. It is no surprise then that the operating cost of maintaining an intensive care unit is a major pain point for hospitals across the country and that innovations are necessary to help drive costs down.

My solution

The basis of IC-U is to quantify a risk value (called the "IC-U" factor) reflecting intensive care urgency for patients that present to a hospital. Additionally, it will ouput potential interventions that may reduce this risk.

Value proposition

On an individual basis, quantifying intensive care urgency will allow hospitals to identify those patients most likely in need of intensive care sooner and suggest potential interventions that may reduce this urgency.

At an aggregate level, the ability to classify patients by apparent intensive care urgency will provide hospitals the ability to optimize patient triaging and monitoring and better manage and predict ICU capacity. Combined, all of these benefits would lead to decreases in mortality and morbidity, reductions in ICU stays and stay durations, potentially more streamlined ICU staffing, and ultimately reductions in operating costs.

How it works

IC-U works as follows:

  1. Hospital staff inputs patient information into a web form
  2. Upon submission, the predictive model analyzes the patient data and compute a risk factor that quantifies the "urgency" that this patient will need intensive care.
  3. Finally, the app displays the results with a description of the main contributors to the IC-U factor, and potential interventions that may be associated with a reduction in the risk factor.

As more information for a patient is gathered, the inputs can be updated and a new IC-U factor can be calculated and displayed as needed.
